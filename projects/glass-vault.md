# GLASS VAULT

## NAME

GLASS VAULT - minimal secure file drop with one-time retrieval links and strict delivery boundaries

## SYNOPSIS

`glass-vault send <asset> --recipient team@example.com --expires 24h`

## DESCRIPTION

GLASS VAULT is positioned as a focused alternative to bulky file-sharing suites. The pitch is simple: when a team needs to transmit a sensitive document, build artifact, or credential bundle, they should not have to invite the recipient into a sprawling workspace or trust a link that can be forwarded indefinitely. The project centers on a temporary vault model where each upload is intentionally constrained by audience, lifetime, and retrieval count. In demo form, that makes the concept easy to communicate. You are not selling generalized collaboration. You are selling deliberate delivery with an emphasis on control and auditability.

The fictional experience begins with a sender dropping in a file and selecting the rules that define the exchange. The sender chooses who can open it, how long it remains available, and whether the download should self-destruct after the first successful retrieval. That narrow set of controls is the heart of the product. The interface should feel calm and exact, not overloaded. The more the system behaves like a sealed transfer envelope, the stronger the idea becomes. The name GLASS VAULT suggests visibility without exposure: the sender can observe the state of the transfer, but the contents stay protected until the intended recipient performs the right handshake.

For a demo audience, the most persuasive angle is operational confidence. Teams constantly move artifacts that are too sensitive for chat attachments and too temporary for a full-blown repository. Legal drafts, configuration exports, emergency access bundles, and customer recovery packages all benefit from a transmission layer that records what happened without turning every exchange into a permanent collaboration surface. GLASS VAULT addresses that gap by making ephemerality a feature rather than a limitation. The transfer has a beginning, a single controlled handoff, and an end.

The concept also allows room for compliance and trust messaging without becoming heavy. Audit entries can record upload time, recipient verification, expiration, and retrieval outcome. Optional watermarking or download disclaimers can reinforce intent without changing the underlying simplicity. Even the failure modes are useful in a demo: expired links, duplicate access attempts, and revoked packages all communicate that the product respects boundaries. That gives the project a mature tone. Instead of pretending every share is frictionless, it acknowledges that sensitive transfers need guardrails, especially when the sender may never speak directly with the recipient again.

A final selling point is how clearly the product can be demoed in under five minutes. Upload, assign recipient, generate link, observe retrieval, watch expiration. That sequence tells a complete story without requiring a large setup or complex training. It also makes the project credible as an internal tool, a customer-facing secure exchange, or a white-label capability inside a larger platform.

## OUTPUT

When successful, GLASS VAULT yields a single controlled delivery event, a concise audit trail, and a clean expiration path that reduces lingering access risk after the intended transfer is complete.

## STATUS

Concept demo. Intended to communicate secure delivery, temporary access, and low-friction handling of sensitive files.
